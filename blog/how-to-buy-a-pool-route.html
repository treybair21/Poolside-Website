How to Buy a Pool Route: A Buyer's Complete Guide

By Poolside Business Brokers · 14 min read

Buying a pool route is one of the smartest ways to enter the pool service industry — or expand an existing operation. Unlike starting from scratch, you acquire an established customer base with verified revenue, proven retention, and immediate cash flow from day one.

This guide walks you through the entire buying process, from evaluating your first listing to closing the deal and servicing your first accounts.

Why Buy a Pool Route Instead of Starting From Scratch?

Building a pool route from zero takes time, marketing spend, and patience. Most new pool service businesses take 12-18 months to build a sustainable customer base. When you buy an existing route, you skip that entire ramp-up period:

  • Immediate revenue — start earning from day one with established accounts
  • Proven economics — you can see actual revenue history, not projections
  • Existing relationships — customers are already accustomed to regular service
  • Established billing — autopay, service schedules, and pricing are already set
  • Lower risk — documented performance reduces uncertainty compared to a startup

Step 1: Define Your Buying Criteria

Before you start looking at listings, get clear on what you want:

Budget

Pool routes typically sell for 10-12x monthly service revenue. A route generating $8,000/month will cost roughly $80,000-$96,000. Be honest about what you can afford, including working capital for the first few months of operation (chemicals, fuel, insurance, equipment).

Location

Where do you want to service accounts? Consider your home base, traffic patterns, and commute. A route that's close to where you live saves fuel and time. If you're new to the industry, start in an area you know well.

Size

For a solo owner-operator, up to 60 accounts is a typical starting point. This provides a full work week with good income. If you plan to hire technicians from the start, routes of 80+ accounts make more sense financially.

Experience Level

You don't need pool service experience to buy a route — many successful owners started with no prior knowledge. However, you should factor in a learning curve. Most sellers provide 1-2 weeks of hands-on training, and industry certifications from organizations like IPSSA and PHTA can accelerate your education.

Step 3: Evaluate the Route

Once you've identified a route of interest, here's what to evaluate:

Financial Performance

  • Monthly and annual revenue — verify the numbers match bank statements or payment processor records
  • Revenue trend — is revenue growing, stable, or declining?
  • Seasonal patterns — understand how revenue fluctuates through the year in that market
  • Average revenue per account — this tells you how the route is priced relative to competitors

Customer Quality

  • Retention rate — what percentage of customers have been on service for 1+ years? 3+ years?
  • Autopay percentage — higher is better, 70%+ is strong
  • Payment history — are there customers with chronic late payments?
  • Service type mix — weekly full-service accounts are the most valuable

Route Characteristics

  • Geographic density — how tightly clustered are the accounts? Can you service the route efficiently?
  • Drive time analysis — total daily drive time impacts your profitability
  • Growth potential — is there room to add accounts in the service area?
  • Pool types — residential vs. commercial, chlorine vs. saltwater, equipment condition

Step 4: Perform Due Diligence

Due diligence is where you verify everything the seller has claimed. Never skip this step:

  • Review financial records — at least 12 months of revenue data, ideally 24 months
  • Verify account count — confirm the exact number of active, paying accounts
  • Ride along on the route — spend a day (or more) riding along with the seller to see the accounts firsthand
  • Check equipment condition — inspect the pools you'll be servicing for any major issues
  • Review customer contracts — understand any service agreements, pricing commitments, or special terms
  • Verify insurance and licensing — make sure you understand the insurance and licensing requirements in your state

Step 5: Finance Your Purchase

Several financing options are available for pool route purchases:

Cash Purchase

The simplest option. You pay the full amount at closing through escrow. This gives you the strongest negotiating position and often results in a lower purchase price.

Seller Financing

Many sellers will finance a portion of the purchase (typically 20-40%) over 6-24 months. This reduces your upfront capital requirement and aligns the seller's interest with a smooth transition — they want you to succeed because they're still collecting payments.

SBA Loans

SBA 7(a) loans can be used for pool route purchases. These typically require 10-20% down with competitive interest rates and longer repayment terms. The application process takes 30-60 days, so start early if this is your financing path.

Equipment and Working Capital Loans

Some lenders specialize in route-based businesses and offer loans specifically structured for this type of acquisition. These can be faster to close than SBA loans.

Step 6: Make an Offer and Negotiate

When you're ready to buy, here are the key terms to negotiate:

  • Purchase price — based on the valuation multiple applied to verified monthly revenue
  • Payment terms — all cash, seller financing split, or third-party financing
  • Training period — typically 1-2 weeks of hands-on training included in the sale
  • Account retention guarantees — protection if accounts cancel within a specified period after closing
  • Non-compete from seller — the seller should agree not to service pools in your area for a defined period
  • Included assets — clarify what's included (customer list, equipment, chemical inventory, software accounts)
  • Transition timeline — when ownership and servicing responsibility transfer

Step 7: Close and Transition

Once terms are agreed:

  1. Sign the purchase agreement — a formal contract covering all terms and conditions
  2. Set up escrow — funds are held by a neutral third party until all conditions are met
  3. Complete training — the seller introduces you to each account, shares route knowledge, and transfers institutional knowledge
  4. Transfer billing — autopay accounts, software access, and customer records are transferred to you
  5. Begin servicing — you start servicing accounts, with the seller available by phone for questions

Tips for a Smooth Transition

  • Meet every customer in person during the training period
  • Take detailed notes on each account: gate codes, chemical preferences, equipment notes, pet warnings
  • Don't change pricing or service schedules immediately — earn customer trust first
  • Over-communicate during the first month — text or email customers after each service to build confidence
  • Address any issues immediately — your responsiveness in the first 30 days sets the tone for the relationship

What Does It Cost to Operate a Pool Route?

Beyond the purchase price, plan for these ongoing costs:

  • Chemicals — typically about 20% of service revenue
  • Vehicle costs — fuel, insurance, maintenance (13-15% of revenue)
  • Equipment — pool poles, nets, brushes, testing kits, pumps ($2,000-$5,000 initial investment)
  • Insurance — general liability and commercial auto ($200-$400/month)
  • Software — route management, billing, customer communication ($50-$200/month)

Is a Pool Route a Good Investment?

Pool routes are one of the strongest small business investments available for several reasons:

  • Recession-resistant — pool maintenance is essential; homeowners maintain pools regardless of economic conditions
  • High margins — 35-55% net margins are standard for well-run routes
  • Predictable revenue — monthly recurring billing provides stable, forecastable income
  • Scalable — start with one route, add accounts organically, or acquire additional routes
  • Low overhead — no office, no warehouse, minimal equipment compared to other businesses
  • Exit value — pool routes retain value and are sellable when you're ready to move on
