How to Sell a Pool Route: The Complete Guide

By Poolside Business Brokers · 15 min read

Selling your pool route is one of the most significant financial decisions you'll make as a pool service business owner. Whether you're retiring, relocating, or simply ready for a new chapter, getting the process right can mean the difference between leaving money on the table and maximizing the value of what you've built.

This guide covers everything you need to know about selling a pool route — from preparation and valuation to finding buyers, negotiating terms, and managing a smooth transition. It's based on our experience transacting over 1,500 pool service accounts across all 50 states.

Step 1: Understand What Your Pool Route Is Worth

Before you list your route, you need a realistic understanding of its market value. Pool routes typically sell for 10 to 12 times monthly service revenue, but the actual multiple depends on several factors:

  • Account density and geography — tightly clustered accounts reduce drive time and increase efficiency, making the route more attractive to buyers
  • Customer retention rate — long-term customers with low churn signal a healthy, well-managed route
  • Autopay penetration — accounts on autopay with consistent payment records are significantly more valuable
  • Service mix — routes with weekly service plus add-on revenue from repairs, equipment, and chemicals command higher multiples
  • Market demand — some geographic markets have higher buyer demand, which drives up valuations

A route generating $15,000 per month in service revenue might sell for $150,000 to $180,000 depending on these factors. The key is getting an accurate valuation from someone who understands pool route economics — not just generic business multiples.

Step 2: Prepare Your Route for Sale

The best time to start preparing your route for sale is 3 to 6 months before you plan to list. Here's what to focus on:

Clean Up Your Financials

Buyers want clean, verifiable financial records. Organize your revenue data by month, document any seasonal patterns, and make sure your billing records match your bank deposits. If you use pool service software like Skimmer, ServiceTitan, or PoolBrain, make sure your records are current and accurate.

Optimize Customer Retention

Every customer cancellation before the sale reduces your route's value. Focus on service quality, address any outstanding issues, and make sure your customers are satisfied. If you have accounts that haven't been serviced recently or have unresolved complaints, deal with those now.

Increase Autopay Enrollment

Buyers pay more for routes with high autopay rates because it signals predictable revenue and reduces collection risk. If you haven't pushed autopay with your customers, start now. Even a 10-15% increase in autopay enrollment can meaningfully impact your sale price.

Document Everything

Prepare a route profile that includes: total account count, monthly and annual revenue, customer list with service details, service area map, equipment list, chemical supply arrangements, and any subcontractor relationships. The more organized your documentation, the faster and smoother the sale process.

Step 3: Choose How to Sell

You have three main options for selling your pool route:

Option 1: Sell Direct (No Broker)

You find a buyer yourself, negotiate the deal, and manage the transaction. This saves the commission but requires significant time, effort, and expertise. You'll need to handle buyer vetting, price negotiation, contract drafting, escrow coordination, and transition planning on your own. Most sellers underestimate how complex and time-consuming this is.

Option 2: Use a General Business Broker

General business brokers handle all types of businesses. They typically charge 15-20% commission and may not understand the nuances of pool route valuations. They'll apply generic small business multiples that may not reflect the true value of a well-run pool route.

Step 4: Find Qualified Buyers

Finding the right buyer is critical. The wrong buyer can lead to extended timelines, failed negotiations, or poor customer retention during the transition. Here's what to look for:

  • Financial capability — the buyer needs to demonstrate they have the funds (or financing) to complete the purchase
  • Operational readiness — do they have the equipment, insurance, and licensing to service the accounts?
  • Relevant experience — while experience isn't required, buyers with pool service knowledge or business ownership experience tend to transition more smoothly
  • Genuine intent — serious buyers move decisively. Tire-kickers waste your time and can delay the sale

When you work with a broker like Poolside, every buyer who sees your listing has already been vetted for financial capability and genuine purchase intent. This dramatically reduces the time to close and eliminates unqualified inquiries.

Step 5: Negotiate the Deal

Once you have interested buyers, negotiation begins. Key terms to consider:

  • Purchase price — the total amount the buyer will pay for the route
  • Payment structure — all cash at closing vs. structured payments (e.g., 70% at closing, 30% over 90 days)
  • Training period — typically 1-2 weeks where you introduce the buyer to accounts and share route knowledge
  • Account retention guarantees — many deals include provisions that protect the buyer if accounts cancel during the transition period
  • Non-compete agreement — buyers typically require that you don't service pools in the same area for a specified period
  • Escrow terms — how funds are held and released during the transaction

A good broker manages the entire negotiation process, advocating for your interests while maintaining a productive relationship with the buyer. This is where experience matters — knowing what terms are standard, what's negotiable, and what red flags to watch for.

Step 6: Close the Transaction

Once terms are agreed upon, the closing process involves:

  1. Purchase agreement — a formal contract outlining all terms, conditions, and timelines
  2. Escrow setup — a neutral third-party escrow service holds funds until all conditions are met
  3. Due diligence period — the buyer reviews all documentation and may ride along on the route
  4. Final walkthrough — buyer confirms account count and condition
  5. Fund release and transfer — payment is released from escrow and ownership transfers

Step 7: Manage the Transition

The transition period is arguably the most important phase of the sale. Poor transitions lead to customer cancellations, which can trigger account retention clauses in the purchase agreement. Here's how to do it right:

Customer Communication

Introduce the buyer to your customers personally. A brief visit where you introduce the new owner goes a long way in retaining accounts. Customers want to know they'll receive the same quality of service.

Route Knowledge Transfer

Share everything you know: gate codes, dog warnings, customer preferences, equipment quirks, chemical sensitivities, billing notes. The more information the buyer has, the smoother the handoff.

Billing and Software Handover

Transfer all billing relationships, software accounts, and customer records. Make sure autopay is transitioned correctly and that no customers fall through the cracks.

Post-Closing Support

Be available by phone for the first few weeks after the transition. The buyer will inevitably have questions about specific accounts, and your responsiveness during this period directly impacts customer retention.

Common Mistakes Sellers Make

  • Overpricing the route — unrealistic pricing delays the sale and can drive away qualified buyers
  • Waiting too long to prepare — poor documentation and messy financials reduce buyer confidence and sale price
  • Selling to the wrong buyer — a buyer who can't afford the route or lacks the capability to service it will create problems
  • Neglecting the transition — a rushed or poorly managed transition leads to customer cancellations that can cost you money
  • Not using escrow — always use a neutral third-party escrow service. Never transfer accounts before payment is secured

How Long Does It Take to Sell a Pool Route?

With the right preparation, pricing, and broker, most pool routes receive qualified offers within 2 weeks of listing and close within 4 to 6 weeks. The full timeline from preparation to closing is typically 2 to 3 months.

Routes that take longer to sell usually have one of these issues: overpricing, poor documentation, low account density, or location in a market with limited buyer demand.
